# 5/3/2026 11 PM - Schematic Design!

_Time spent: 6h_

Apr. 23

Today, I spent a few hours in KiCad drafting the wiring schematic. Since im a beginner, it took a bit of time figuring out where to route each signal and understanding the net logic required for the MCU to communicate with the peripherals. My initial attempt to manually route wires from point to point was proven unfeasible because of the risk of signal noise, so I pivoted to a 4-layer PCB.


![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777850415215-mu9gm0.png)


Short Circuits: I accidentally connected the battery power directly to the ground. If I hadn't caught that in the software, the 1.5Ah battery would have shorted out immediately.

Floating Pins: I left the EN pins disconnected. I learned that you can't just leave pins pins floating, and that they need to be tied to a specific voltage (3.3V) or the chip won't know whether to turn on or stay off.

The 3.3V Trace: I initially thought I needed a whole layer for power, but I realized I could just route 3.3V as a normal trace since my ground plane was taking up the bulk of the space for heat management.

Power Dips: I added capacitors (C1 and C2) because I learned that when the motor kicks on, it can suck out enough power to reset the entire board, so the capacitors can make sure that this doesn't happen.

Now that the schematic is done, the next thing to do is design the PCB!

# 5/3/2026 10 PM - Hardware preparation and selection

_Time spent: 4.25h_

Apr. 19th

Today I chose all the hardware I was going to use for this project.

Firstly, spent some time time researching the ESP32-S3 versus the ESP32-C3. While the S3 is a powerhouse, its more than I need and would affect my battery life. So, instead, I am going to use the ESP-32C3. It’s much smaller, which helps with the devices actual size, and its deep-sleep power consumption is exactly what I need to keep the internal clock running while the rest of the board sleeps. 

![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777847196686-1f2iq0.png)

However, the C3 has a thermal belly pad, so I wouldnt be able to solder it conventionally. I will probably need to use a hot plate and do some reflow soldering.

For the haptics, after doing some research, I found that most normal vibration motors have a muddied feel, so, for user experience, I am going to LRA haptic motor along with the DRV2605L haptic driver, which has a built-in library of effects and patterns and can control the haptic motor precisely.

The biggest pivot of the day, however, was the battery. I initially looked at a tiny 110mAh LiPo to keep things sleek, but after doing the math, I found that there was a safety issue. Standard charging chips like the TP4056 output 1A by default, which is 45 times the 22mAh charge rate of the 110 mAh LiPo! I want this to be THE BOMB and not A BOMB, so I’ve decided to go with a 1500mAh (1.5Ah) LiPo battery.

Next, i'll make my wiring schematic on KiCAD.

   If you have ADHD, you have probably been affected by "time blindness" more than a few times. For my neurotypical brothers out there, time blindness is the difficulty with perceiving, estimating, and managing time. It causes chronic lateness, the underestimating of task times, and crazy procrastination (for more information, you can read about it [here](https://add.org/adhd-time-blindness/).) I want to make an ESP-32-powered accessory that can help "nudge" me and oathers back into the real world. 

Since my AP's are coming up and I have a crazy amount of self-studying to do, I toggled an accesibility feature on my laptop to verbally announce the time every fifteen minutes. However, since it is a audibly announced, it sometimes does not register in my head and I lose track of time again. I want my device to be more subtle, but have a more potent effect. So, my device should stimulate the user every so often to give them a passive perception of the time. 


![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777260546982-l4tquq.png)


To do this, I have decided to aim for a pocket-sized vibrating device that buzzes in a custom time interview. The device should be light, have a good battery life, and generate a noticable simulus. Also, I am thinking of leveraging the full potential of such a device by making it multifunctional. I could utilize diferent vibration patterns or strengths to indicate different things. For example, a hydration reminder could be a heavy, one second vibration, while the regular time reminder could be a short small chirp.

I made a small sketch on what the device would look like and what features I want on it. 

![image](https://stasis.hackclub-assets.com/images/1777262095844-vbcje2.png)

No. 1 - Vibration Motor

I want to use a vibration motor vs. a speaker or something similar because they are relatively quiet but also provides a physical stimulus to really pull me down to earth.

No. 2 - Battery

The device obviously has to be portable, and to keep power, it needs a good internal battery that can last a convenient amount of time.

No. 3 - Clean Build

I want the device to be visually clean so I wont be stuck with an eyesore in my every day life.

No. 4 - Button for sleep

This one is very important. Since I want to keep a good battery life as well as interact with the device without plugging into the USB-C port, turning off the bluetooth, WiFi, and haptic feedback when I am sleeping or do not need it is a really great way to cut down on the power consumption. However, I cannot just turn off the board since I need the ESP-32's clock to be accurately calibrated. So, this sleep button would turn off the main functions of the device while keeping the internal clock running.

No. 5 - WiFi for configuring

When I want to configure the buzzes times, patterns, and frequencies, as well as recalibrate the clock in the case of the battery completely dying, I want to be able to connect to the device on an online portal and update it.

As I mentioned before, I want the buzzes to only be felt by the user that has the device in their pocket, as to avoid distracting other people or drawing uneccesary attention. So, the quieter the vibration motor I can find, the better.

No. 7 - Pocket Sized

The device should fit in the user's pocket with ease and be lightweight.

No. 8 - Sturdy

As someone who drops their phone a lot, I want the device to be as sturdy and drop-resistant as possible to avoid damage. Somewhat related, I usually lose track of time in the shower and if I could somehow make the product water-resistant then that would be great.
